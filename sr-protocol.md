## 1. Background

### 1.1 Clinical Burden

Rib fractures are the most common thoracic injury in trauma, occurring in approximately 10% of all trauma admissions and 55% of blunt chest trauma patients. In the United States alone, over 350,000 people sustain rib fractures annually, with more than one-third requiring hospitalization. The global burden is substantial: trauma accounts for nearly 10% of all deaths worldwide, and chest trauma contributes to or causes up to 25% of trauma mortality.

Older adults (≥65 years) represent a rapidly growing proportion of trauma patients. Rib fracture incidence in this population has increased by 52% over the past decade, with patients aged ≥65 now accounting for approximately 50% of all rib fracture hospitalizations. Age-related physiological changes — including osteoporosis, decreased chest wall compliance, reduced pulmonary reserve, and higher comorbidity burden — place older adults at disproportionately high risk for complications. Mortality from rib fractures in patients ≥65 years ranges from 9% to 22%, compared to 4-10% in younger cohorts, with each additional rib fracture increasing the odds of mortality by 19% and pneumonia by 27%.

### 1.2 Current Standard of Care

Non-operative management remains the cornerstone of treatment for rib fractures in older adults. Standard care includes multimodal analgesia (oral and intravenous analgesics, regional anesthesia techniques such as epidural analgesia, paravertebral blocks, and serratus anterior plane blocks), incentive spirometry, noninvasive positive pressure ventilation, and early mobilization. However, despite optimal medical management, complication rates remain high, with pneumonia developing in 15-31% of elderly patients and up to 51% requiring ICU admission.

### 1.3 Evidence Gap

Surgical stabilization of rib fractures (SSRF) — also known as rib fixation or rib plating — has emerged as an alternative to non-operative management. Multiple systematic reviews and meta-analyses have demonstrated that SSRF reduces mortality, pneumonia, duration of mechanical ventilation, and length of stay in mixed-age adult populations, particularly in patients with flail chest. However, these reviews have focused on all adults, with age-specific analyses limited to subgroup analyses.

The Eastern Association for the Surgery of Trauma (EAST) published a 2023 practice management guideline on non-surgical management and analgesia strategies specifically for older adults with multiple rib fractures, but this guideline explicitly excluded surgical management. Conversely, the 2017 EAST guideline on operative fixation of rib fractures addressed all adults without age-specific considerations. To date, no systematic review has focused specifically on the comparative effectiveness of SSRF versus non-operative management in patients aged ≥65 years.

This gap is clinically important because the risk-benefit calculus for surgery differs fundamentally in older adults: they face higher baseline mortality and complication risks from non-operative care, but also have higher surgical risk due to comorbidities, frailty, and reduced physiological reserve. A dedicated synthesis of the evidence in this population is needed to inform surgical decision-making and guideline development.

### 1.4 Previous Reviews

- EAST guideline (2017): Operative fixation of rib fractures after blunt trauma — mixed-age population, no geriatric focus
- EAST guideline (2023): Non-surgical management in older adults — explicitly excluded surgical management
- Craxford et al. (2024): Meta-analysis of RCTs on SSRF — all adults, no age-specific analysis
- Shiroff et al. (2022): SSRF systematic review of severe rib fractures — included subgroup analysis by age but not focused on elderly
- Beks et al. (2019): SSRF for flail chest — mixed-age population

## 2. Objectives

### 2.1 Primary Objective

To determine the effect of surgical stabilization of rib fractures compared to non-operative management on mortality in adults aged 65 years and older with traumatic rib fractures.

### 2.2 Secondary Objectives

1. To compare rates of pneumonia between SSRF and non-operative management
2. To compare intensive care unit and hospital length of stay
3. To compare duration of mechanical ventilation and tracheostomy rates
4. To compare pain scores and quality of life outcomes
5. To evaluate the incidence of surgical complications
6. To explore effect modification by age subgroup (65-74, 75-84, ≥85), fracture pattern (flail vs non-flail), and timing of surgery (early vs late)

---

## 3. Eligibility Criteria

### 3.1 Inclusion Criteria

| Domain | Inclusion |
|--------|-----------|
| Population | Adults ≥65 years with traumatic rib fracture(s) of any number and any mechanism (blunt or penetrating) |
| Intervention | Surgical stabilization of rib fractures (any technique: plates/screws, intramedullary devices, K-wires, or other operative fixation) |
| Comparator | Non-operative management (analgesia, regional anesthesia, respiratory therapy, NIPPV, or any combination) |
| Outcomes | At least one of: mortality, pneumonia, ICU/hospital LOS, mechanical ventilation duration, tracheostomy, pain, QoL |
| Study design | RCT, quasi-RCT, prospective cohort, retrospective cohort, case-control, before-after study with ≥10 patients |
| Language | English |

### 3.2 Exclusion Criteria

| Domain | Exclusion |
|--------|-----------|
| Population | Age <65 years without separate data for ≥65 subgroup; pathologic or non-traumatic fractures |
| Intervention | Single-arm surgical series without comparator group |
| Study design | Case reports (n<10), narrative reviews, systematic reviews, editorials, commentaries, cadaveric/animal studies, conference abstracts without full text |
| Publication | Duplicate publications of the same cohort (use most complete or recent) |

## 5. Screening Process

### 5.1 Title and Abstract Screening

Two independent reviewers will screen titles and abstracts against eligibility criteria using a standardized screening form. A pilot test of 50 records will be conducted to calibrate reviewers, with a target inter-rater agreement (Cohen's kappa) of ≥0.70.

### 5.2 Full-Text Screening

Records passing title/abstract screening will undergo full-text review by two independent reviewers. Reasons for exclusion at this stage will be documented.

### 5.3 Disagreement Resolution

Disagreements at either stage will be resolved by consensus discussion or by a third reviewer if consensus cannot be reached.

### 5.4 Screening Tool

Records will be managed using a systematic review screening tool (Rayyan, Covidence, or spreadsheet-based tracking).

---

## 6. Data Extraction

A standardized extraction form will be developed and piloted on 5 studies.

### 6.1 Variables to Extract

- **Study characteristics**: Author, year, country, design, setting, enrollment period, sample size, funding
- **Population**: Age (mean, median, distribution), sex, injury severity score (ISS), number of rib fractures, flail chest proportion, comorbidities, mechanism of injury
- **Intervention**: Surgical technique (plates, intramedullary, other), timing of surgery, surgeon specialty
- **Comparator**: Non-operative protocol details (analgesia type, respiratory therapy, ICU admission criteria)
- **Outcomes**: Mortality (in-hospital, 30-day), pneumonia, ICU LOS, hospital LOS, mechanical ventilation duration, tracheostomy, pain scores, QoL measures, surgical complications

### 6.2 Extraction Process

One reviewer will extract data using the standardized form. A second reviewer will verify extracted data for accuracy, with full dual extraction for the primary outcome (mortality).

## 7. Risk of Bias Assessment

| Study Design | Tool | Domains |
|-------------|------|---------|
| RCTs | Cochrane RoB 2 | 5 domains: randomization, deviations, missing data, outcome measurement, selective reporting |
| Observational (cohort, case-control) | ROBINS-I | 7 domains: confounding, selection, classification, deviations, missing data, measurement, selective reporting |
| Before-after studies | ROBINS-I (modified) | As above |

Two independent assessors will evaluate each included study. Disagreements resolved by consensus or third reviewer. Results presented as summary tables and traffic-light plots (using robvis).

## 8. Statistical Analysis Plan

### 8.1 Effect Measures

- Dichotomous outcomes (mortality, pneumonia, tracheostomy): Risk ratio (RR) with 95% CI
- Continuous outcomes (LOS, ventilation days): Mean difference (MD) or standardized mean difference (SMD) with 95% CI
- Time-to-event: Hazard ratio (HR) with 95% CI

### 8.2 Primary Analysis

Random-effects meta-analysis using restricted maximum likelihood (REML) estimation. If REML fails to converge, DerSimonian-Laird will be used. Results presented as forest plots.

### 8.3 Heterogeneity Assessment

- I² statistic (thresholds: 0-40% may not be important, 30-60% moderate, 50-90% substantial, 75-100% considerable)
- Cochrane Q test (p<0.10 considered significant)
- τ² (between-study variance)
- 95% prediction interval reported for all pooled estimates with ≥3 studies

### 8.4 Subgroup Analyses (Pre-specified)

1. Age: 65-74 vs 75-84 vs ≥85 years
2. Fracture pattern: Flail chest vs non-flail multiple fractures vs single/isolated
3. Timing of surgery: Early (<72 hours) vs late (≥72 hours)
4. Study design: RCTs vs observational studies

### 8.5 Sensitivity Analyses

- Excluding studies at high/critical risk of bias
- Fixed-effects model (as sensitivity to random-effects assumption)
- Leave-one-out analysis
- Excluding studies with industry funding

### 8.6 Publication Bias

Funnel plot asymmetry assessed by Egger's regression test if ≥10 studies are included. If asymmetry is detected, trim-and-fill analysis will be performed to estimate adjusted effect size.
